There are many parallels between {% intlink parametrized_surface_introduction %}parametrized surfaces{%endintlink%} and {%intlink parametrized_curve_introduction%}parametrized curves{%endintlink%}. Both are parametrized by vector-valued functions, with the difference being the dimension of their inputs (one-dimensional $t$ for parametrized curves and two-dimensional $(\spfv,\spsv)$ for parametrized surfaces). Both have two possible orientations (oriented by choice of {%intlink parametrized_curve_orient %}tangent vector{%endintlink%} for curves and by choice of {%intlink parametrized_surface_orient %}normal vector{%endintlink%} for surfaces). Both have integrals giving their measure ({%intlink parametrized_curve_arc_length %}arc length{%endintlink%} or {%intlink parametrized_surface_area%}area{%endintlink%}) in terms of derivatives of the parametrization that describe how the domain is stretched and shrunk as it is mapped onto the image.

In the same way, surface integrals are similar to {% intlink line_integral_scalar_function_introduction%}line integrals{%endintlink%}. Just as there are two types of integrals over curves (line integrals of {% intlink line_integral_scalar_function_introduction%}scalar functions{%endintlink%} and of {% intlink line_integral_vector_field_introduction%}vector fields{%endintlink%}) there are two types of surface integrals: surface integrals of scalar functions (discussed on this page) and {% intlink surface_integral_vector_field_introduction%}surface integrals of vector fields{%endintlink%}. The surface integral of a scalar function is a simple generalization of a {% intlink double_integral_introduction%}double integral{%endintlink%}. Like the {% intlink line_integral_vector_field_introduction%}line integral of vector fields{%endintlink%}, the {% intlink surface_integral_vector_field_introduction%}surface integrals of vector fields{%endintlink%} will play a big role in the fundamental theorems of vector calculus.

Let $\dls$ be a surface parametrized by $\dlsp(\spfv,\spsv)$ for $(\spfv,\spsv)$ in some region $\dlr$. Imagine you wanted to calculate the mass of the surface given its density at each point $\vc{x}$ described by the scalar-valued function $f(\vc{x})$. The mass will be the integral of the density over the surface (just like the mass of a wire was the {%intlink line_integral_scalar_function_introduction%}integral of a density{%endintlink%} over the curve).

If the surface happened to be lying in the $xy$-plane rather than floating in three-dimensional space, the situation would be identical to that of {% intlink double_integral_change_variables_introduction%}changing variables in double integrals{%endintlink%} (other than the region we call $\dlr$ here is called $\dlr^*$ in that context changing variables). We need only slight modifications for the case of a surface floating in three dimensional space.

To calculate the mass, we integrate $f(\dlsp(\spfv,\spsv))$ as $(\spfv,\spsv)$ range over the domain $\dlr$. In order to multiply the density $f$ by area on the surface not by area in $\dlr$, we must account for any stretching or shrinking by $\dlsp$ as it maps $\dlr$ onto the surface, as illustrated in the below applet.

{% boxedapplet parametrized_helicoid_surface_area %}

We already calculated the stretching or shrinking by $\dlsp$ when we calculated {%intlink parametrized_surface_area %}surface area{%endintlink%}. The area of the region on the surface that is the image of a small $\Delta\spfv \times \Delta\spsv$ rectangle in $\dlr$ is \begin{align*} \Delta A = \left\| \pdiff{\dlsp}{\spfv}(\spfv,\spsv) \times \pdiff{\dlsp}{\spsv}(\spfv,\spsv) \right\| \Delta \spsv \Delta \spsv. \end{align*} If we multiply this by the density $f(\dlsp(\spfv,\spsv))$, we will obtain the mass of that small section. We could approximate the mass of the whole surface by a Riemann sum of such terms. Then, by letting $\Delta\spfv$ and $\Delta\spsv$ go to zero, we would discover that the mass of the whole surface is the integral \begin{align*} \ssint{\dls}{f} = \iint_\dlr f(\dlsp(\spfv,\spsv))\left\| \pdiff{\dlsp}{\spfv}(\spfv,\spsv) \times \pdiff{\dlsp}{\spsv}(\spfv,\spsv) \right\| d\spfv\,d\spsv. \end{align*} This integral is a two-dimensional analog of the {%intlink line_integral_scalar_function_introduction%}line integral of a scalar-valued function{%endintlink%} except that $\|\dlsp'(t)\|$ is replaced by \begin{align*} \left\| \pdiff{\dlsp}{\spfv}(\spfv,\spsv) \times \pdiff{\dlsp}{\spsv}(\spfv,\spsv) \right\|. \end{align*}
